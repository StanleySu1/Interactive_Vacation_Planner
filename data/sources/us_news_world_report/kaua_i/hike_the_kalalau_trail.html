How we rank things to do

Many people come to Kaua'i with high hopes of backpacking the 22-mile round-trip Kalalau Trail. This trail is so popular because it is the only land-based option to explore the world-famous Napali Coast. No roads or paved streets go along, in or through this famous coastline.

The hike takes travelers through five valleys in Napali Coast State Wilderness Park, passing through lush tropical forests, bubbling streams, cascading waterfalls and dramatic cliffside ocean views. What's more, the hike ends at the remote Kalalau Beach. But access to this unworldly scenery comes with a price. Novice hikers, expert expeditioners, the Hawaii Tourism Authority and state officials all say the same: This hike is a lot of work and shouldn't be attempted without the proper equipment and, most importantly, experience. The trail is filled with elevation and terrain changes throughout. Trails are not wide and open, but rather narrow, rocky and very muddy. And once you hit Crawler's Ledge around mile 7, the dirt path starts to disappear and the rock climbing – or rather clinging – begins.

Even those who attempt the first 2 miles of the trail to Hanakapi'ai Valley, the popular abbreviated version of the hike, still describe the trek as strenuous. There is no shortage of rocky inclines, narrow sections and the most prominent feature: mud. The mud is a big reason why so many urge travelers to come prepared. Proper hiking shoes with solid traction and a hiking pole or staff are strongly advised. What's more, there are no formal restrooms or drinking water along the trail, but you will find composting toilets at the 2-mile, 6-mile and 12-mile markers.

You will have to cross at least one stream to complete any part of the hike, whether it's the full trail or the first 2 miles. Streams are normally quite shallow and filled with rocks big and small that travelers choose to traverse to get across. Sounds easy enough, but officials say streams are dangerous parts of the trail. That's because the stream rises often unexpectedly since this is one of the rainiest spots on earth, causing a surge of water that becomes dangerous to cross. If you are hiking and see the water start to rise, do not cross the stream; officials say doing so can be deadly. If you see a stream starting to rise, wait for it to go down. The Division of State Parks does not provide emergency services for the trail; in case of an emergency, someone will have to hike out to find help or flag down a passing boat or helicopter. (There's no cellular phone coverage in the area.)

The trailhead for the Kalalau Trail and Hanakapi'ai Beach and waterfalls is now subject to daily visitor limits and require advanced reservations to enter Ha'ena State Park, where the trailhead is located.

Pricing

Reservation fees vary depending on how you plan to access Ha'ena State Park. If you're planning to drive yourself, you'll pay a $10 parking fee, as well as a $5 entry fee per person. If you'd like to take advantage of the round-trip shuttle from Hanalei, it costs $35 per adult and $25 per child ages 4 to 15 (children 3 and younger enter for free). Anyone proceeding beyond Hanakapi'ai must have a valid camping permit (available 90 days in advance). Entry and parking reservations are required from Ke'e Beach to Hanakapi'ai (2 miles) and camping permits are required for Hanakapi'ai to Hanakoa (4 miles).

Hours

Day use of the park is available from 7 a.m. to 7:45 p.m. daily. Visit the Hawaii State Park's website for more information on how to hike the Kalalau Trail.
