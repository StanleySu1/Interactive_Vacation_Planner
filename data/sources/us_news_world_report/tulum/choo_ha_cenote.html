How we rank things to do

Part of a trio of cenotes near the ancient Mayan archaeological site of Cobá, Choo-Ha’s inviting crystal clear waters draw families in particular because of its swimmable, shallow waters. Surrounded by a lush garden, the freshwater sinkhole is bordered by limestone rock formations and hanging stalagmites that welcome visitors to wade through waters that Mayans once considered sacred. A visit to Choo-Ha can be coupled with two neighboring cenotes, Multun-ha and Tankach-ha, which share the same ticket office. 

What recent travelers say

Travelers call Choo-Ha a worthwhile day trip from Tulum, especially when paired with a visit to Cobá. Many described it as a “hidden gem” with fewer crowds compared to some of the area’s more well-known cenotes. Reviewers note the wooden stairs that descend into the water are manageable and lead to crystal blue water, though you may want to consider bringing water shoes to protect your feet and provide grip as the stairs can be slippery. For those who would prefer larger cenotes with platforms to jump off of, the bigger Tankach Ha is your best bet, according to reviewers. 

Choo-Ha is about 35 miles north of Tulum. Once in the area, drive past the Cobá archaeological site and note the sign marked “Cenotes Coba” on the road. If arriving by car, there is parking on the premises. Once inside, there are changing facilities and restrooms. 

Hours and Tickets

The entrance fee for Choo-Ha is 100 pesos (about $6) and it’s open daily from 9 a.m. to 8 p.m. Though not required, life jackets are available to rent for 50 pesos (about $3).
