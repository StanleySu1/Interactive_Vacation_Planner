U.S. News Insider Tip: Can’t get enough gangster history? End your tour with a drink at the Ohio Club, Arkansas’ oldest bar and a former popular hangout for Al Capone and others. – Justine Harrington

Prohibition-era gangsters like Al Capone, Frank Costello, George “Bugs” Moran and Charles "Lucky" Luciano are often associated with cities like Chicago and New York – considered the epicenter for the various crime outfits of that time period. But did you know that Hot Springs was also a key destination for gangsters during Prohibition? One of the most fascinating local attractions in Hot Springs is the Gangster Museum of America, where you can learn about the city’s (shall we say, colorful) gambling history and how it acted as a safe haven for gangsters fleeing law enforcement in Chicago and New York City.

Museum highlights

Several galleries showcasing artifacts and photos from the ‘20s, ‘30s and ‘40s offer visitors an immersive look at the historical events that defined these eras and, most notably, the notorious gangsters that reigned during this time – and who vacationed in Hot Springs, including Capone, Costello and Moran.

Along with antique casino equipment and Prohibition-era artifacts purchased from collectors, you’ll also see video presentations featuring eyewitness accounts from former law enforcement agents.

Visitors routinely report enjoying the informative tour guides who do an excellent job of bringing the city’s history to life. Thus, the museum is recommended for anyone who likes learning about local history, not just those interested in American gangster culture.

Hours and Tickets

The Gangster Museum of America is open Sunday through Thursday from 10 a.m. to 5 or 6 p.m. and Friday and Saturday from 10 a.m. to 6 or 7 p.m., depending on the season. General admission tickets, which included a guided tour of the museum, cost $16 for adults, $15 for seniors and $6 for kids ages 8 through 12; children 7 and younger get in free.

The museum is conveniently located downtown amid all the action on Central Avenue. For more information, visit the Gangster Museum of America website.
