How we rank things to do

Photographers, wildlife watchers, and history buffs will all enjoy a stop at the Mormon Row Historic District. Listed on the National Register of Historic Places, this tract of land was homesteaded by Mormon farmers starting in 1895. In the late 1800s and early 1900s, "Mormon Rows" were settled across the West; this particular Mormon Row is the most intact one still in existence. At one point, there were 26 homesteads on Mormon Row. Today about 30 log and timber-frame barns, houses and outbuildings still stand on what were six homesteads.

GTNP and the GTNP Foundation, a nonprofit that works to steward, protect and enhance the park, have been working together since 2021 to stabilize and preserve the remaining buildings of Mormon Row, and to add interpretive elements that will help visitors understand the area’s history. The project is expected to be finished in 2027.

Those who want to get photos of what is arguably the most iconic view of the Tetons – the range rising behind the district’s T.A. Moulton barn – don’t have to wait until the preservation project is completed. The barn is there, just as it has been since Thomas Alma Moulton built it between 1913 and 1939.

If you're lucky, you might see the Jackson Hole bison herd grazing in the sagebrush flats around Mormon Row. In June, these same flats are abloom with arrowleaf balsamroot, a cheerful yellow flower that adds to the scenery. To increase your chances of spotting wildlife in this area, several past travelers recommended arriving around sunrise or sunset.

Mormon Row is on Antelope Flats Road, which is off U.S. Highway 191/26/89, roughly a mile north of Moose Junction in the park's southeastern swath. Drive east on Antelope Flats Road for a little more than a mile to a small parking lot near a pink stucco house, which was part of the John Moulton homestead. Mormon Row is in an area of Grand Teton National Park that is accessible without paying the park’s admission fee, so visiting it is entirely free. Brochures are available in the parking area. Check out the National Park Service's website to learn more.
