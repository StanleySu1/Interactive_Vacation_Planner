How we rank things to do

This 1,106-acre preserve on the eastern shore of Phelps Lake and within Grand Teton National Park has an interesting history. In 1908, the valley’s first dude ranch was founded on land that today is part of the preserve. In 1932, John D. Rockefeller, Jr., who was instrumental in the founding of the GTNP that we know today, bought it. His original intention was to donate it to the park, but he instead made it into a Rockefeller family retreat, named the JY Ranch. The ranch became part of the park in 2007 when John’s son Laurance gave the land to GTNP (after numerous homes and roads were removed to restore the land). The Preserve Center and trails opened to the public in 2008.

If your legs are tired from hiking the park's steep trails, you're in luck: some of the flattest hiking trails in GTNP are here. Lake Creek – Woodland Trail Loop is a 3-mile hike to Phelps Lake. For hikers looking for a challenge, try routes to Death Canyon or Granite Canyon. Visitors say the hike to Phelps Lake is a nice walk with stunning scenery throughout, especially at the lake. Formed by retreating glaciers during the last ice age, Phelps Lake sits at the mouth of Death Canyon and is 160 feet deep; benches on its shore invite you to sit and admire the view.

You don’t have to hike to enjoy this refuge though. The 7,000-square-foot Preserve Center, which was the first building to achieve LEED-Platinum status in both the U.S. National Park Service and Wyoming, feels more like a chapel than a visitor center, given its location. Here there is a resource room with books, maps, leather chairs and a fireplace as well as exhibits highlighting John D. Rockefeller’s vision for the preserve, his legacy of conservation stewardship and the plants wildlife present in the area.

The Laurance S. Rockefeller Preserve Center is typically open from early June to late September from 9 a.m. to 5 p.m. daily. There are extended hours in the summer. When it is open, you'll want to get there early. Parking is limited to 50 cars and once the parking lot is full, there’s a one car in, one car out policy. This means that the preserve is never crowded. It also means that if you don’t arrive when it first opens, you’ll likely have to wait for a parking spot. This is especially true in July and August.
