How we rank things to do

Upon your arrival into Sedona via State Route 179, you'll be greeted by the iconic bell-shaped rock formation known – appropriately enough – as Bell Rock. It's a popular first stop for visitors, as the landscape is likely to take your breath away. No matter what time of day you arrive, the lighting on the majestic red-hued mountains is ever-changing. In the background you can spot the "Rabbit Ears" rock formation in the distance, which makes for an excellent photograph, especially at dawn or dusk.

The 3.6-mile trail tends to get busy, as it is one of the most popular destinations for Sedona travelers. There are a few trails that connect to Bell Rock, including Courthouse Butte, where hikers can take a nearly 4-mile loop around both monoliths.

Bell Rock is also one of the four major Sedona vortexes. For decades, travelers from around the world have visited Bell Rock for meditative reflection and inspiration. It's believed that this full-energy vortex is inspires serenity.

A lot of people have the urgency to want to hike up Bell Rock, however, it's not necessary to go to the top of Bell Rock. In fact, in can be quite dangerous, even for experienced climbers, who have noted that the climb down is much more dangerous and difficult than the climb up. The first and second tiers are the safest, and they offer spectacular views.

The perimeter of Bell Rock and Courthouse Butte is relatively flat and is perfect for mountain bikers who want to get a lay of the land. It's also a great place for equestrians, as horses are allowed on the trail. There is a designated parking space for horse trailers at the trailhead.

Taking the Baby Bell trail is another hidden gem. The views of Cathedral Rock, especially during sunset are spectacular. It's also a great spot for photographers during the summer months, especially when the Milky Way is in full view. Be sure to bring a headlamp or flashlight on the way back, as it does tend to get dark and you could lose your way.

Bell Rock is located about 5 miles south of the downtown area off State Route 179. From State Route 89A and the 179 intersection, you will travel south about 3.6 miles to the trailhead parking lot near milepost 309. A Red Rock Pass is required to park at the trailhead. It costs $5 per vehicle per day. For more information, visit the U.S. Forest Service website.
