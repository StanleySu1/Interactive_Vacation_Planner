U.S. News Insider Tip: If you drive through Cromwell, known as the Fruit Capital of New Zealand, be on the lookout for roadside fruit stands and stores like Mrs Jones' Fruit Stall – where browsing for peaches can be interrupted by staff whisking you out back for free fruit port and 7th Heaven wine tastings. – Leena Tailor, Contributor, Travel

New Zealand is home to award-winning wines, and where better to enjoy them than the postcard-worthy places they're produced in? Even nondrinkers can enjoy the alluring settings of regions like Central Otago and Marlborough, where wineries like Giesen Wines and Ara offer nonalcoholic wine.

The island's northern sunny, coastal climate produces pinot noir, chardonnay and sauvignon blanc at vineyards like Moutere Hills and Kahurangi Estate. In the northeast lies the "sauvignon blanc capital" of Marlborough. The region's first vines were planted in the 1880s and thanks to sunny days and cool nights, the area has produced award-winning sauvignon blanc, characterized by gooseberry and capsicum flavors. Cloudy Bay, Brancott Estate Wines and Yealands Wines are popular stops and many tourists have commented on being pleasantly surprised by the food, so leave time for lunch. While tours are available, the region is bike-friendly, with 30 wineries within a 15-mile circuit. Marlborough is also home to the annual Marlborough Food and Wine Festival held every February and part of the Classic New Zealand Wine Trail – 236 miles of cycle trails spanning four wine regions and 100-plus cellar doors.

The Waipara Valley, which sits about 35 miles north of Christchurch on South Island's east side, is known for pinot noir and riesling at wineries like Pegasus Bay, Black Estate, Waipara Hills Wine and Greystone Wines – a favorite among tourists for its  quiet atmosphere, wine flights and food. Tour companies like Waipara Wine Tours and Giles Tours offer round-trip transportation from central Christchurch.

The Waitaki Valley, which is located about 125 miles north Dunedin International Airport, continues emerging as a winemaking region thanks to producers like River-T Wines and Q Wine.

Queenstown also offers ample wine-tasting opportunities. Gibbston Valley has evolved into an all-purpose facility with cellar door, cheese shop, wine cave, concerts, accommodations and meals you'll be craving months later. Nearby, Kinross represents several boutique wineries, making it convenient for sampling various local brands at one stop. Amisfield is where Prince William and Kate Middleton loved the 2011 RKV Reserve Pinot Noir so much that the princess reportedly offered to help pick grapes.

Over in Wanaka, which sits about 45 miles northeast of Queenstown, wineries like Rippon and Maude Wines serve tasty tipples alongside spectacular lake views.

Around 55 miles east of Queenstown, the Central Otago region is home to world-famous pinot noir. Grapes are part of the spellbinding scenery in subregions like Cromwell, Bannockburn and Alexandra. Cycling is one option with the Pedal 4 Pinot trail, while the self-guided 4 Barrels Walking Wine Trail lets tourists hop from one tasting room to the next at their own leisure. However, it's hard to leave the peaceful patio of Mt Difficulty, where wine-tastings are accompanied by striking views of the brand's namesake mountain framed by lush vines and azure skies. Carrick Winery is another popular stop, while farther south, actor Sam Neill (of the "Jurassic World" franchise) can be found tending to grapes at his vineyard Two Paddocks. And if walking, driving or cycling between tasting rooms doesn't appeal to you, fear not. Luxury lakefront hotel and apartment complex The Rees Hotel offers a Heli Wine Tour package that includes a private Central Otago helicopter wine tour guided by a hotel wine expert.
